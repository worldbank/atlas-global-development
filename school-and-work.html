The impact of early education on work prospects

Intro

The goal of [goal: 4] is to ensure inclusive and equitable quality education and promote lifelong learning opportunities for all.

Education can drive growth and cut poverty. Around 2/3rds of the difference in income between high income and low-and-middle income countries can be explained by differences in the human capital.[footnote: Jedwab et al. (2023); Hendricks and Schoellman (2018, 2023)] And roughly 1/3rd of the improvements in extreme poverty since 1980 can be traced to better education.[footnote: Gethin (2025)]

[emphasis: 2/3rds] of differences in income between high income and low and middle income countries comes from human capital. Around half of learning used in a career is acquired at school and the other half at work.

Schools build foundational skills that will be used in a career, [target: 4.1]. But they are only half the story. Nearly half of the skills people use in their careers are learned on the job, [target: 4.4].[footnote: Jedwab et al. (2023), Schady et al. (2026).] This story looks at both the learning that is taking place in the classroom –or not taking place – and the learning that continues at work.

Learning at School

Schooling has expanded dramatically. The Barro-Lee dataset combines national census and survey records to estimate average years of schooling for adults over time. By harmonizing data across nearly 150 countries and multiple decades, it creates a consistent picture of educational attainment from 1870 to today. This makes it possible to track long-run trends—how countries expanded schooling and converged (or diverged) in educational outcomes over the past century.

These changes have been driven by improvements in completion rates, most notably for primary school, but secondary school completion rates have improved as well. In 2000, a child in a low-income country had only a 50% chance of finishing secondary school, with girls at a disadvantage. By 2020, the odds had risen to 70%, and gender gaps had nearly closed.

improvement in the chance that a child completes secondary school between 2000 and 2020.

How does Schooling Impact Jobs

Few economic policies consistently yield double digit returns at scale. Education is one of them. Education not only affects the earnings for those who work, it increases the likelihood of employment and the type of job, too.

In low- and middle-income countries, education shapes employment opportunities. Men with college education are about 5 percentage points more likely to work than those with little schooling, while for women the gap is even larger at 16 points. Education also influences the type of job: wage workers typically have 2.5 more years of schooling than the self-employed, and wage jobs tend to deliver faster earnings growth over time.

Women who go to college are

16  percentage points

more likely to work than those with less than basic education

The payoff shows clearly in wages. In Brazil, workers with secondary education earn nearly twice as much as those with little schooling. With a university degree, the premium rises to 167%. Similar patterns hold in Georgia, India, the Philippines, and South Africa.

Looking across countries, the earnings boost from an extra year of schooling varies widely—reflecting differences in school quality, labor markets, demand for skills, and measurement, but the returns are consistently positive. There is no clear link between a country’s income level and the return to education. High returns to education can be found in both high and low-income countries. On average across countries, each additional year of schooling raises earnings by about 10%.

These returns can easily pay off the modest expenditures on education made in many countries. In Sub-Saharan Africa, the average expenditure on a year of school per child is around $283.[footnote: Source: Education Finance Watch (2024)] If we assume the return to a year of schooling is around 10% and we apply this to average earnings of $1726.65 in Sub-Saharan Africa and a work career spanning 40 years with a 5% discount of future earnings, this implies an improvement of discounted future earnings of around $2,962.43[footnote: Average earnings per year for Sub-Saharan Africa calculated using data from World BanK PIP. Series: Survey mean/average consumption or income per capita, total population (2021 PPP $ per day)]. This implies a return of roughly ten times the initial investment in that year of schooling.

Across countries, each additional year of schooling boosts earnings by about 10%. Looking backwards and assuming the returns are roughly constant over time, between 1920 and 2025, average schooling rose from 1.6 to 9.2 years. Applying this 10% return implies that global earnings roughly doubled because of improvements in the years of schooling.

Learning Quality

But the quantity of schooling doesn't tell the full story. Students in high-income countries tend to learn more per year of schooling than those in poorer countries. Adjusting for learning, gaps are even larger. Improving education quality could unlock far greater gains.

Using Harmonized Learning Outcomes (HLO)[footnote: See Kraay (2019) for a description of the harmonized learning outcomes (HLO)] to adjust for learning, Learning‑Adjusted Years of Schooling (LAYS) among 25–34 year‑olds are on average almost three times higher in high‑income than in low‑income countries.[footnote: Schady et al. (2025)]

In Mali, the years of schooling are low at around 2.7 years. However, these 2.7 years of schooling are equivalent to the learning that takes place after only 1.3 years in an advanced system. The Philippines and Brazil both have a bit more than 9 years of school for the average adult. However, learning-adjusted years of schooling are only 5.3 and 6.3 years respectively. South Korea has a relatively small gap between the years of schooling and learning-adjusted years of school, indicating relatively high learning at school. Improving learning quality, especially where attainment has expanded, would amplify returns to schooling.

How do Jobs Impact Learning

Learning in formal classroom settings is a critical driver of human capital accumulation. But the classroom is not the only place where people learn. Learning also happens in the workplace, where skills are not only utilized but acquired[target: 4.4].

Each year of work experience increases productivity by around 2.5% per year. Moreover, individuals tend to spend more time working than in schooling. Globally, the average person has around nine years of education. Careers often span forty years or more. Learning in the workplace explains an additional 1/3rd of the difference in income between high income and low or middle income countries.[footnote: Jedwab et al. (2023)]

Skills learned on the Job

Gaps in learning acquired either through formal schooling or on the job leads to large differences in the skills available in the workplace, particularly digital skills. SDG 4.4.1 measures the proportion of youth and adults with information and communications technology (ICT) skills. Taking one aspect, whether or not an individual knows how to use electronic presentation software, only around 5% can do so in Côte d'Ivoire, 13% in Brazil, while 41% can in France.

of people in Côte d'Ivoire can use electronic presentation tools.

Learning on the job

Labor force surveys allow us to go beyond simple averages and see how earnings evolve with age and experience. Increases in wages—at least when looking at averages across workers—can be interpreted as a measure of skills gained on the job, since wages tend to reflect productivity. By breaking down wages across cohorts, we can observe not just how schooling raises earnings, but how learning continues in the workplace over the course of a career. This shows how education and experience interact to shape lifetime earnings trajectories.

The pattern shows two forces: higher initial entry wages for the more educated and steeper experiential wage growth, so that experience amplifies (rather than narrows) education-based disparities over the lifecycle.

Why is this the case? In many cases, workers with lower levels of education work in jobs where little to no learning takes place. In low and lower middle income countries around 69% of workers are self employed. As discussed in Schady et al. (2025), these workers tend to accumulate around half as much human capital as workers in wage employment. Further, many more workers are in agriculture, domestic, or manual occupations or micro firms, which have less technology exposure, learning from peers or managers, or explicit workplace training. Occupational choice is closely tied to education levels, and so gaps in earnings between highly educated and lesser educated workers tend to expand over time.

66 %

of workers in low and middle income countries are farmers, self-employed, or wage workers in micro-firms where less skill improvement takes place.

Summary

Education is one of the key drivers of economic growth and poverty reduction. While a substantial portion of what is learned is done so at school, learning at work explains around half of the human capital acquired at the end of a career.

There have been considerable improvements in educational attainment over the past century, rising from around one year of schooling on average to close to nine years of schooling by 2025. This progress has substantially increased the earnings of workers over time. However, more work is needed to increase completion rates, particularly for secondary education and higher education. While progress has been made on increasing completion rates, education quality remains a major issue.

At work, many workers, particularly the least educated, are in jobs where little learning takes place. Around two-thirds of workers in low and middle income countries are in jobs where productivity and wages increase only a small or negligible amount. Improvements in education and learning at work show great promise in improving living standards around the world, but much work remains.
