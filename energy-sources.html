Phasing out fossil-fuel subsidies: can it be done?

Phasing out fossil-fuel subsidies: can it be done?

The remarkable economic progress the world has experienced until now has largely been powered by fossil energy. The increased levels and variety in consumption are also anthropogenic factors contributing to the record levels of global warming evident in our world today. SDG 12 calls for responsible means of production and consumption that ensure sustainable development in the long run for all people on the planet. This story focuses on the real political and geopolitical issues that have undermined progress in the removal of fossil-fuel subsidies around the world and suggests reforms to address these issues.

Introduction

This chapter focuses on [target: 12.c]: “Rationalize inefficient fossil-fuel subsidies that encourage wasteful consumption by removing market distortions…phasing out those harmful subsidies…to reflect their environmental impacts, taking fully into account the specific needs and conditions of developing countries…”

Governments face tough choices in allocating scarce budgets—balancing investments in infrastructure, health, education, and other priorities. Yet political pressure has often pushed them to subsidize fossil fuel consumption, especially during recent price surges when crude oil rose from about USD 40 a barrel in 2021 to over USD 120 in 2022.

The scale is striking: in 2022, governments spent one dollar on fossil fuel subsidies for every three dollars spent on education ([goal: 4]), and total subsidies exceeded six times the official development aid provided by high-income countries ([goal: 17]). While some of this surge reflected temporary responses to the energy crisis, it underscores the vast fiscal space consumed by fossil fuel subsidies.

Why subsidies are harmful

[emphasis: Inefficient resource allocation. ]Fuel subsidies distort prices, encouraging excessive fossil fuel use at below-market costs. This misallocation reduces efficiency and delays investment in cleaner alternatives [reference: coady1].

[emphasis: Fiscal burden. ]Subsidies strain public budgets, diverting funds from health, education, and infrastructure. In many low- and middle-income economies, subsidy costs exceed spending on essential services [reference: imf1].

[emphasis: Economic inequality. ]Although often presented as pro-poor, subsidies disproportionately benefit wealthier households, who consume more fuel. Studies consistently show their regressive nature and impact on inequality [reference: arze1].

[emphasis: Corruption. ]Fuel subsidies are prone to corruption and smuggling. In Nigeria, their removal in 2023 revealed how cheap fuel had fueled cross-border leakages, a problem also documented elsewhere [reference: musa1].

[emphasis: Environmental damages. ]Subsidies lock in fossil fuel use, driving greenhouse gas emissions that undermine SDG 13. They also intensify air pollution, congestion, and traffic accidents, imposing heavy social and environmental costs [reference: iea1].

Efficient pricing of fossil fuel

Fossil fuel use imposes large external costs—local air pollution, global warming, road congestion, and accidents—that are rarely reflected in retail fuel prices [reference: black1]. The efficient price of fuel includes supply costs, these externalities, and applicable consumption taxes. When retail prices fall below this level, governments not only subsidize production but also forgo valuable tax revenue. For example, if the efficient gasoline price is $1.25 per liter (supply $0.50, external costs $0.60, VAT $0.15) but the pump price is $0.30, society bears the hidden costs through fiscal losses and environmental damages.

Why subsidies are hard to remove

Subsidies persist due to intersecting political, economic, and social reasons.

[emphasis: Macroeconomic dependence:] In resource-rich countries, subsidies are seen as citizens’ share of resource wealth. Removing them breaks the social contract. Example: Nigeria’s 2012 reform attempt sparked nationwide strikes and a partial rollback [reference: worldbank2022].

[emphasis: Political economy:] Oil companies, miners, transport unions, and allied elites resist reform to protect profits, often with government ties. Example: Indonesia’s 2005–08 reforms faced union and elite pushback [reference: iea1].

[emphasis: Protests:] Subsidy cuts raise costs for food, transport, and energy, triggering unrest. Example: Ecuador’s 2019 subsidy removal led to mass protests and reversal [reference: imf2023].

[emphasis: Unfair green transition:] Industrialized nations have built wealth on fossil fuel and are now home to growing, energy-intensive data centers powering the AI-revolution. Yet poorer countries face pressure to quit fossil fuels, thus weakening compliance [reference: mit1], [reference: reuters1].[emphasis: ]

Reforms

Reforms aimed at eliminating fossil fuel subsidies often fail. This is the outcome of a lack of good strategy and political will. Previous reforms around the world suggest what works when designing energy reforms.

[emphasis: Price Adjustment & Phasing: ]Gradual increases (e.g., India’s monthly diesel hikes); automatic pricing tied to global oil (e.g., Morocco, Kenya).

[emphasis: Targeted Social Protection: ]Cash transfers (Indonesia, Iran); Redirect subsidies to the poor (India’s LPG DBT); Lifeline tariffs: cheap quota, market rates beyond.

[emphasis: Transparency & Communication: ]Campaigns showing who really benefits (mostly wealthy); Show savings reinvested (schools, health, roads); Leverage peer examples to build public acceptance; Example: Ghana publicized fuel vs. health spending.

[emphasis: Fiscal & Policy Reallocation: ]Reinvest savings in education, healthcare, and infrastructure; Fund renewables and public transport to cut fossil reliance.

[emphasis: Institutional Reforms: ]Independent pricing bodies to depoliticize; Liberalize markets to reduce monopoly reliance; Publish regular subsidy cost/beneficiary reports.[emphasis: ]

[emphasis: Timing & Sequencing: ]Reform during low oil prices (Morocco, 2014); Pair with safety nets before hikes; Start with non-essential fuels first.

Conclusion

The consumption of fossil fuels drives climate change and local air pollution, yet governments continue to spend heavily on subsidies—rising from USD 0.5 trillion in 2020 to USD 1.3 trillion in 2022, partly to reduce suffering during the energy crisis.[reference: black1]

Progress on subsidy removal is [emphasis: moving backward]: it is among the[emphasis: 33%] of SDG targets that have stalled or regressed.[reference: un2] Though reforms have lacked political support, evidence from middle-income countries suggests that reforms would likely be successful to reduce fossil fuel subsidies, if the resources would be channelled into the provision of infrastructure, education, and healthcare.[reference: hoy]

A full reform in the pricing of fossil fuel (i.e., incorporating all supply and environmental costs) is estimated to reduce global CO2 emissions by approximately 43 percent below baseline levels by 2030, raise government revenue globally by about 3.6 percent of global GDP, and prevent about 1.6 million premature deaths per year from local air pollution.[reference: black1]
