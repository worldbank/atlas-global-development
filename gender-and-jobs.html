Shaping the future: Women’s work, unpacked

Shaping the future: Women’s work, unpacked

Historically, women have faced significant barriers to full participation in society. Until relatively recently, prevailing social norms confined women’s roles to the private sphere, excluding them from the labor market and other income-generating opportunities.[reference: social-norms] Over the past century, however, many women have entered the formal workforce, though the pace and extent of progress vary widely across countries.

While workforce participation is primarily measured under [goal: 8] (Decent Work and Economic Growth), it remains a central dimension of [goal: 5] (Gender Equality), underscoring the critical link between women’s economic participation and broader gender equality goals.

Globally, around 8 in 10 men between the ages of 15 and 64 are part of the labor force. [reference: wdi-lfp] But what about women? Take a guess in the chart below and see how close you come to the actual share.

Women’s participation in the labor force

Since 1990, women’s participation in the labor force has remained largely unchanged, with only half of the women between the ages of 15 and 64 engaged in paid work. This represents a substantial gap compared to men—roughly 30 percentage points.[reference: wdi-lfp]

Regional patterns tell a more nuanced story. In some regions, women’s participation has increased noticeably. But progress has been uneven; in several regions, the gap remains striking.[reference: wdi-lfp]

391 more years before equality

Progress in increasing women’s participation in the labor force has been extremely slow. Progress varies widely across countries and tends to move at different speeds depending on their stage of development. To put this into perspective, consider the trajectory of a “typical” country.

So, what contributed towards the increase in women’s participation in Turkiye’s labor force?

Drivers for increasing women’s labor force participation

Several factors came together to shape women’s participation in Türkiye’s labor force. Beginning in the 1990s, the economy underwent a structural transformation, shifting away from agriculture and toward industry and services. Many women had been engaged in agriculture as unpaid family workers, but as farms shrank, families migrated to cities.[reference: turkey-driver]

Opportunities available in urban labor markets often demanded new skills and formal training, and childcare support was limited. As is common in the early stages of industrialization, men were more likely to acquire the new skills needed, while women—faced with lower levels of education and lack of childcare—were excluded from these opportunities. With little opportunities available to them, many women withdrew from the workforce.[reference: turkey-driver] Between 1990 and 2004, women’s labor force participation fell from 36 percent to 25 percent.[reference: wdi-lfp] ​​As education levels rose, younger generations of women began to join the labor market in greater numbers, finding opportunities in industry and services.[reference: turkey-driver]

At the same time, social norms and family dynamics were evolving.[reference: turkey-driver] Fertility rates in Türkiye declined faster than the global average, reaching 1.5 by 2023.[reference: wdi-fertility] With fewer children, the demand for childcare eased somewhat, opening up opportunities for more women to consider paid work.[reference: turkey-driver]

Education played an equally important role: while women’s participation in the labor force has historically been lower than men’s, the gap is widest among those with little or no education, and narrowest among those with advanced degrees.[reference: ilo-education] As more women gained access to tertiary education, their opportunities in the labor market expanded.

From the 2000s onward, these shifts—falling fertility, rising education levels, and gradual changes in social norms—combined with economic growth helped create more jobs for women.[reference: turkey-driver] As a result, female labor force participation began to rise again, reaching 41 percent in 2024.[reference: wdi-lfp] Still, this progress has only been possible where social attitudes allowed women the freedom to work and where economic conditions created opportunities in the labor market.[reference: social-norms]

Shifting social norms, preventing child marriage, expanding childcare support, and promoting equitable division of domestic responsibilities are therefore key drivers in enabling more women to enter and thrive in the labor market, both as employees and entrepreneurs.

Women represent a vast untapped source of productivity, innovation, and growth.[reference: unwomen-work] Increasing female labor force participation allows societies to fully reap the returns on investments in women’s education and health.

In many countries, removing barriers to women’s participation in the labor force could raise output by 15-20% and produce strong income gains.[reference: gender-distortions] Yet, we continue to see a misallocation of talent: Women’s skills and potential are not used efficiently because social, legal, or institutional barriers prevent them from accessing the most productive jobs, or the job market altogether. As a result, the economy produces less output than it could if all talent were fully and optimally utilized. So, even when they are employed, women face greater and more complex challenges. These include the types of jobs available to them and the way their work is valued and compensated.[reference: gender-distortions]

Vulnerable employment

To explore these aspects of work, let’s have a closer look at the labor force in two very different countries. Switzerland and Tanzania have similar rates of labor force participation for both women and men but different types of employment patterns.[reference: ilo-employmenttype] In simple terms, the labor force includes everyone who is either working or unemployed.[reference: active-work]

Contributing family workers and own-account workers are considered in vulnerable employment because they may lack contracts, social protection, and savings. A common example of an own-account worker is a street vendor, while contributing family workers are often agricultural workers.

Vulnerable employment is often mixed with but also overlaps with the common notion of informal employment. Informal work includes work in the informal economy (unregistered enterprises and/or making no social security protections), informal work in the formal economy (nondeclared jobs, earnings below social protection thresholds, or non-compliance with law), and informal work in households (contributing family workers). While informal employment is about the informality of working arrangements, vulnerable employment represents economic insecurity.[reference: ilo-vulnerable-informal]

People in vulnerable employment are highly exposed to economic shocks and are most likely to fall into poverty - and as in the case of Tanzania, they are predominantly women.[reference: ilo-employmenttype] Such high rates of vulnerable employment among women are not unique to Tanzania, but reflect a broader trend seen across many low- and middle-income countries.[reference: vulnerable-employment]

Vulnerable employment is widespread in low-income countries, where rates can reach as high as 90 percent. In these settings, not only is the majority of the workforce in vulnerable jobs, but women are disproportionately affected. The gender gap in vulnerable employment tends to be widest in poorer countries—particularly those with higher fertility rates and larger young-age dependency ratios. Discriminatory laws related to marriage and parenthood further limit women’s access to secure work. [reference: vulnerable-employment]

By contrast, in high-income countries—where legal and social barriers to employment are weaker—vulnerable employment is far less common. People in these economies typically attain higher levels of education and therefore have greater access to stable and secure jobs. Interestingly, in many high-income countries, men are slightly more likely than women to be in vulnerable employment. While there is limited research explaining this pattern, one possible explanation is that this concept is difficult to measure consistently across countries. At such low levels of vulnerable employment, the indicator may capture self-employed professionals such as accountants or attorneys rather than own-account workers like street vendors, leading to some distortion in the data.

Gender wage gap

Disadvantages for women persist beyond the type of employment and also in the value of their work, as reflected in persistent wage gaps. Several factors drive the gender gap in earnings, including occupational segregation, women’s underrepresentation in leadership, and disparities in education and skills.[reference: wage-gap]

Women are often concentrated in lower-paying sectors such as domestic work, clerical services, and contributing work in farms, while men are more likely to hold jobs in higher-paying fields like industry, technology, or formal wage employment. Even within the same sector, women are less likely to occupy supervisory or leadership positions, limiting their earning potential.[reference: occu-seg]

Gaps in education and skills—shaped by unequal access to schooling, training, and professional networks—further constrain women’s opportunities to move into higher-paying work. Restrictive social norms also reinforce these barriers by limiting women’s participation and advancement in the workforce even after receiving education.[reference: occu-seg]

Yet, even after accounting for these differences, a portion of the earnings gap remains unexplained.

Narrowing the gender wage gap benefits not only individual women and their economic well-being, but also society as a whole. On average, countries could see a 14 percent increase in wealth by closing the gap in lifetime earnings between men and women.[reference: wage-wealth]

Such patterns of disadvantages for women in the work force whether in the type of employment, the employment arrangement or the value for their work point to structural inequalities in labor markets that limit women’s economic security and constrain broader development outcomes.

Unpaid work

Discussions of employment usually focus on work outside the household. However, a substantial share of productive activity consists of unpaid domestic and care work carried out within households.

Domestic and care work refers to food preparation, cleaning and upkeep of a dwelling, shopping, childcare, and care of the sick, elderly or disabled household members, among others.[reference: gdp-timespent] This work is important in daily life and contributes to human capital development and productivity; but it is not equally divided between women and men.

Why do women shoulder a disproportionate amount of unpaid work? Specific reasons vary by family but often include social attitudes and cultural traditions that dictate gendered division of labor, economic inequalities including the gender pay gap, and lack of social services such as childcare. When access to childcare services is expanded, more women can join the labor force and those already in employment are able to work more hours.[reference: childcare-laborforce]

Formalizing and valuing the work that women perform outside the labor market could have an economy-wide triple effect. A conservative estimate of the total value of care work provided by women is $11 trillion, equivalent to 9 percent of the global Gross Domestic Product (GDP).[reference: carework-value]

Unequal burden of unpaid care and domestic work, which limits women’s time and opportunities for formal employment, is yet another constraint faced by women. However, with the changing labor force market itself, women’s participation may be further impacted.

Future of jobs

As artificial intelligence (AI) reshapes labor markets globally, the future of jobs remains complex and evolving. Globally, there are pronounced differences in AI exposure between women and men largely driven by women’s overrepresentation in sectors prone to AI adoption such as clerical work, administration, and services.

The gender gap in AI exposure exists across income levels but is largest in high and upper middle income countries where AI penetration is comparatively more advanced. In low income countries, AI exposure is still nascent given shortfalls in internet penetration and access to electricity.[reference: ai-exposure]

Women’s jobs in developing countries are less susceptible to AI disruption because a significant share of women remain employed in agriculture and the informal sector. To ensure women are not left behind in the AI-driven economy, countries must close the gender digital divide.

Increased AI exposure can have different effects on jobs: displace, enhance or create new jobs. This will play out differently for different groups of women. In high-income countries, almost 10 percent of employed women work in jobs considered to be at the highest risk of AI-driven task automation compared to only 3.5 percent of men.[reference: genai-jobs] Similarly, in middle income countries, women are more exposed than men to the AI displacement effect, for examplein Malaysia and the Philippines.[reference: futurejobs-ai] As digitalization deepens in low income countries, women’s risk of displacement, especially in service sectors may grow unless proactive measures are taken.

But the diffusion of AI-enabled technologies can also benefit female employment. For example, in Europe, a 10 decile rise in AI exposure corresponds to a 2.2 – 2.9 percent increase in female employment share (more than double that for the total employment share). The largest positive effects on female employment from AI adoption are seen in countries where women have made greater educational progress and participate more in the labor market.[reference: ai-women-europe]

To adapt to the changing demand in skills, countries at all income levels need to scale up reskilling efforts focused on AI-related competencies.[reference: ai-unesco]
