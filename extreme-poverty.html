Measuring extreme poverty

The objective of [goal: 1] is to end poverty in all its forms everywhere, with[target: 1.1] aiming to eradicate extreme poverty by 2030. Extreme poverty is defined as the proportion of people living below the international poverty line, currently set at $3.00 per person per day.[reference: foster2025] This reflects the typical poverty line in the world’s poorest countries, and having less than $3.00 per person per day is associated with not satisfying basic needs like food and shelter. $3.00 may seem very low, but many households—both historically and today—fall below the line.

Extreme poverty is best measured from surveys that capture consumption or income patterns from a representative sample of households in a country. Such surveys are not carried out annually in all countries, but under strong assumptions about how national economic growth reaches the poor, data from the World Bank’s Poverty and Inequality Platform can be interpolated, extrapolated back to 1950, and extrapolated forward to 2025.[reference: mahler2025a][reference: mahler2025b][reference: worldbank2025a] These extrapolations carry significant uncertainty, particularly for the earlier decades where survey data is limited. Nevertheless, this modelling provides a useful approximation of long-term trends in global poverty.

Extreme poverty around the world since 1950

In 1950, an estimated 60% of the global population lived in extreme poverty. Since then, this share has steadily declined, and today, approximately 10% of people worldwide live in extreme poverty.

All regions have experienced reductions in poverty over this period, with the most significant decline seen in South and East Asia. Latin America and the Caribbean, as well as the Middle East and North Africa, also achieved substantial declines—particularly in the earlier decades. However, in recent years, extreme poverty has been rising again in the Middle East and North Africa. Sub-Saharan Africa has also made progress, though at a slower and less consistent pace. Over the past decade, poverty rates in the region have stagnated, and close to 50% of its population still lives in extreme poverty.

Behind these global and regional trends are diverse national experiences. A major driver of the overall decline in poverty has been from countries that have had periods of accelerated economic growth, transforming them from being entrenched in poverty to having nearly or completely eliminated it.[reference: hausmann2005] China’s trajectory is a well-known example, but the global reduction in poverty cannot be attributed to China alone. South Korea, for instance, also exemplifies a remarkable transformation.

The next 25 years

Where does this leave global poverty going forward?

Most of the countries that made rapid progress in reducing poverty over the past few decades have nearly or completely eradicated extreme poverty. As a result, they no longer contribute significantly to further declines in the global poverty rate.

Meanwhile, many of the countries that are currently home to large populations of poor people have seen little or no progress in the last decade. The eight countries with the highest numbers of people living in extreme poverty account for about half of the global total. Among these, only India has achieved substantial reductions in the last decade. Three—Democratic Republic of the Congo, Pakistan, and Tanzania—have seen modest declines, while four—Nigeria, Ethiopia, Mozambique, and Sudan—have experienced increases in poverty.

The countries where extreme poverty has risen over the past decade now account for more than half of the world’s extreme poor. By contrast, between 1980 and 2019, 85%-95% lived in countries that had made progress over the preceding decade.

What the future holds is unclear. Instead, we can explore how global poverty will evolve if countries follow their current paths or the paths other countries have taken over the past decades.

The dire projection from countries continuing on their current paths arises because the global speed of progress in reducing poverty is at a 75-year low.[reference: mahler2025a] A key reason for this slowdown is the several large, low-income countries—mentioned earlier—that have made little or no progress over the past decade.

Yet even if all countries reduce extreme poverty, global extreme poverty could still rise. This can happen because population growth is much higher in poorer countries than in richer ones. Currently, countries with extreme poverty rates above 10% are growing their populations at nearly 2.5% per year, while those with poverty rates below 10% grow at just 0.5% per year.

This wasn’t always the case. At the turn of the century, the population growth rate in poor countries was about twice that of richer ones. But as the gap widens, the global population is shifting more rapidly toward regions with higher poverty. This demographic shift means that even if poverty rates fall within these countries, the absolute number of people living in extreme poverty globally may still rise—potentially reversing past progress.

This does not imply that population growth itself has to be a problem. Countries with high population growth may find themselves with a youth dividend — economic growth resulting from a large influx of skilled youth entering the labor market. It does require, however, that these countries manage to create opportunities and jobs for a rapidly growing workforce.

Why are some countries stuck in poverty?

To understand how the fight against global poverty can be reignited, we need to understand why some countries successfully managed to eliminate extreme poverty while others remain entrenched in extreme poverty.

Countries stuck with low growth and high poverty often face compounding challenges hindering sustained progress. They lack quality jobs and the economic growth that such jobs entail. They suffer from low levels of human capital—poor health and low education levels—making it difficult for people to earn a decent living.[reference: cunningham2024] Countries stuck in poverty also tend to have sparse financial resources, preventing the implementation of social safety nets that allow households to alleviate the consequences of poverty and invest in productive assets for their future.[reference: worldbank2025b]

But why have some countries managed to escape these compounding challenges while others have not? There are many possible root causes.[reference: berg2012] Here we relay three partial explanations.

Several countries suffering from high levels of poverty without signs of improvement are experiencing active conflict, which often destroys assets and livelihoods.[reference: corral2020] Countries with high poverty and little progress also have poor economic management, such as poor debt management, preventing governments’ ability to make the long-term investments necessary to spearhead development.[reference: worldbank2024]. Third, many public sectors are characterized by high corruption or low levels of transparency and accountability, leading governments to fail to deliver basic services to their citizens and create opportunities for economic flourishing.[reference: worldbank2017]

Defining countries in conflict, with poor economic management, or facing high levels of corruption in the public sector as those in fragile and conflict-affected situations or those with low scores in Country Policy and Institutional Assessment, we can overlay these root causes with lack of progress on curbing extreme poverty. Of the 44 countries that are not on a path to curb extreme poverty to 10% by 2050, all but 11 suffer from conflict, poor economic management, or high levels of corruption. Seven countries—Afghanistan, Haiti, Mozambique, South Sudan, Sudan, Somalia, and Yemen—suffer from all three combined.

These three root causes are certainly not all relevant factors. Of the countries not suffering from one of the three causes some, such as Kenya, Pakistan, and Vanuatu, are seeing their livelihoods threatened by climate change.[reference: hallegatte2016]. Others, such as several countries in Southern Africa, face some of the highest levels of inequality in the world. High inequality levels imply that some segments of society are born into worse circumstances, facing lower chances of climbing the economic ladder, leading to a waste of human potential and as a consequence a slowdown in growth and poverty reduction.[reference: cuevas2022]

Without addressing these root causes, it will be difficult for many countries to end extreme poverty, and difficult for the global poverty trend to continue its impressive 75 year pathway.

Beyond extreme poverty

Even if substantial progress will be made in reducing global extreme poverty, it is important to keep in mind that the international poverty line used for [target: 1.1] represents an austere definition of poverty. Countries that have ended extreme poverty often suffer from other definitions of poverty.

Middle- and high-income countries tend to use higher poverty lines for their national contexts, reflecting a higher cost to cover what they consider basic needs.[reference: jolliffe2016] Countries also often have multidimensional measures of poverty, which go beyond income or consumption and capture whether individuals are deprived in other aspects of well-being, such as health, education, and infrastructure. These monetary and non-monetary national definitions of poverty are part of [target: 1.2]. They generally reveal greater presence of poverty than the international poverty line does.

Conclusion

Over the last 75 years, the world has witnessed impressive progress in reducing extreme poverty, largely due to countries that have managed in quick periods of time to go from high levels of poverty to virtually eliminate it. Yet progress has slowed down, and several countries appear stuck in poverty due to the presence of conflict, poor governance, high debt levels, and more. Unless these challenges are confronted and resolved, the prospect for the future is bleak, and extreme poverty is likely not to reduce much further.

For the countries that have ended extreme poverty, it is vital to remember that [goal: 1] calls for ending poverty in all its forms, and though extreme poverty has been curbed, other forms of poverty may still be all too present.
