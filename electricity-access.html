Introduction

[target: 7.1]aims to ensure universal access to affordable, reliable, and modern energy services, with Indicator 7.1.1 specifically tracking access to electricity.

Access to electricity is a basic human right[reference: banga]. Lack of access to electricity severely limits opportunities to learn, work, and thrive. Reliable electricity access is important not just for improved comfort and quality of life, but also for supporting work productivity, connectivity, education, and the provision of health services. It enables students to study after dark, ensures storage and provision of vaccines and medical supplies, powers irrigation, enables modern industrial production, and job creation [reference: job_creation] – all essential drivers of economic and social development. Yet, 666 million people still live without electricity access, and countless others have limited or unreliable supply.

Understanding SDG 7.1.1

Indicator - access to electricity

SDG 7.1.1. Indicator[reference: sdg7_1_1] is defined as the proportion of population with access to electricity. This indicator measures the share of people whose primary source of lighting at home is electricity, including grid connections, solar home systems, mini-grids, or other stand-alone renewable systems.

This indicator captures only the most basic level of electricity access. The presence of a household connection does not necessarily mean that electricity is sufficient in quality, reliability, or affordability.[reference: mtf] Nonetheless, achieving universal access at this fundamental level remains an essential first step toward ensuring that everyone benefits from modern energy services.

The primary data sources are nationally representative household surveys and population censuses [reference: esmap_data]that ask whether a household has access to electricity.

Progress and disparities in access to electricity

Global and regional trends in electricity access (percentage)

Global access to electricity has continued to rise over the past two decades, but progress has been uneven. While many regions have achieved near-universal access, large gaps remain elsewhere.

Global, regional, and country trends in people without electricity (number)

The number of people without access to electricity worldwide has declined sharply for the past 24 years, but substantial gaps remain across regions and countries.

Urban-Rural disparities

Despite this remarkable progress, large disparities remain across regions and between urban and rural populations.

There are significant gaps in electricity access between urban and rural populations worldwide. In 2000, about 94 percent of the urban population had access, compared to only 66 percent of the rural population. By 2023, rural access had risen to 84 percent, narrowing the global urban–rural gap about 13 percentage points.

However, regional disparities persist. While most regions have seen steady convergence between urban and rural access levels, Sub-Saharan Africa remains an exception: in 2023, only one-third of rural residents had access to electricity, compared to 82 percent of urban residents.

Country’s progress

Among the 30 countries with the largest populations lacking electricity, 24 are in Sub-Saharan Africa, including the 14 countries with the greatest electricity access deficits. Three countries—[emphasis: Nigeria], [emphasis: the Democratic Republic of Congo], and [emphasis: Ethiopia]—alone account for roughly one-third of the global population without electricity.

The progress score also shows slower advancement in Nigeria and the Democratic Republic of Congo. Between 2015 and 2023, the pace of progress in electricity access in both countries was about half the average rate (scores of 0.59 and 0.55, respectively). By contrast, Ethiopia has been progressing faster than the average (score of 1.8).

We will examine the countries with the largest populations without electricity: Nigeria (87 million), the Democratic Republic of the Congo (80 million), and Ethiopia (56 million).

Obstacles to electrification in Sub-Saharan Africa

Electrification has advanced rapidly across the world since 2000, yet millions of people still lack basic access to electricity—most of whom live in Sub-Saharan Africa. Particularly, rural populations in the region, continue to lag far behind.

Several factors hinder progress toward universal electrification in Sub-Saharan Africa: financial distress among utilities, weak regulatory frameworks, high capital costs and limited access to financing, unsustainable business models of power system operators, and the region’s challenging geography and dispersed rural populations.

Mission 300: Bridging the energy access gap

To help overcome these obstacles and close the electricity access gap, Mission 300 was launched in 2024 by the World Bank and the African Development Bank. It aims to provide access to 300 million people—halving the number of those without electricity in Sub-Saharan Africa by 2030. The initiative promotes investments, policy and regulatory reforms, and private capital to expand grids, improve utility performance, and enhance regional energy integration. Through National Energy Compacts, countries identify key reforms and priorities to expand grid connections, strengthen utilities, and deploy decentralized renewable energy—such as mini-grids and solar home systems—to reach remote populations. Off-grid and mini-grid solutions are emphasized as the fastest and most cost-effective way to extend access in rural and sparsely populated areas while reducing reliance on diesel and supporting climate goals.
