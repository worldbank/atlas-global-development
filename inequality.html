Measuring inequalities

The objective of [goal: 10] is to address and reduce economic, social, and political inequalities within and between countries. [target: 10.1] tracks the income growth among the bottom 40 percent of the population and the total population within countries. If the bottom 40 percent grows at a faster pace than the average of the entire population, global progress is shared more equally.

The Prosperity Gap

The Prosperity Gap captures how far a society is from a prosperity standard of $28 per person per day (in 2021 PPP$), which is approximately the average income when countries reach high-income status. [reference: kraay2023]. The Prosperity Gap brings together the notions of progress (measured by growth in average income) and improvements in equity (measured by the reduction in inequality in society) into one indicator of inclusive growth. The prosperity gap is a decomposable measure, which allows for breaking global progress into progress by regions or countries or to assess the prosperity gap within a country by different subgroups. Furthermore, the prosperity gap puts emphasis on the bottom of the global income distribution, thereby providing more insights on the population groups of particular importance to the World Bank vision and mission.

Trends in Prosperity Gaps

Simulations

Changes in a society's Prosperity Gap are driven by changes in average income or inequality. To see this, consider first what happens to the Prosperity Gap if every Colombian’s income increased by 1 percent? Since income increases proportionally for everyone, the relative inequality remains unchanged and hence all the change in the Prosperity Gap is driven by the change in mean income.

Next, consider what happens if every Colombian gets an extra $1 per day? This would increase both the mean income and reduce the relative inequality in the country, since 1 additional dollar matters relatively more for a poorer person. Both changes contribute to a reduction in the Prosperity Gap.

We can compare for any country the changes in the Prosperity Gap from an equal absolute increase in income – which changes both mean income and relative inequality – and an equal relative increase in income – which changes only the mean income while keeping relative inequality unchanged.
