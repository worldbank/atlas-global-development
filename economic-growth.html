Economic Growth

[target: 8.1] calls for sustained economic growth. Over the course of the past decades the global economy has experienced several setbacks. But it has always managed to bounce back. This includes the years of the COVID-19 pandemic, which caused the largest decrease in the global gross domestic product (GDP) per capita in recent times, albeit countered by a significant increase in subsequent years. As such, the global economy seemed poised to experience average economic growth rates in the 2020s similar to that of previous decades. However, the global economy is now facing substantial headwinds emanating largely from an increase in trade tensions and heightened global policy uncertainty [reference: worldbank2025a]. These headwinds are likely to hit with varying intensity across countries with the poorest countries likely to suffer the most.

SDG 8.1. calls for

7 percent

annual growth in gross domestic product (GDP) of the least developed countries (LDCs).

Since the late 1980s, the World Bank has classified countries by their income level into four groups; low income, lower-middle income, upper-middle income and high income. When looking across these four groups of countries, as they were defined in 1990, the average per capita GDP growth over the next three decades is actually fairly similar. The group of countries that were defined as low-income in 1990 have experienced the highest average annual growth rate in their per capita GDP since then. Despite this, the growth rates are far from the SDG target of 7 percent per year.

If we take a closer look at the growth rates of the countries classified as low-income in 1990, it becomes evident that averages hide a great divergence. The positive story of low-income countries experiencing larger growth than the rest of the world turns out to have more nuances. In fact, while some countries have experienced impressive growth, others have experienced little or no growth, and some countries have even regressed during this period.

The uneven path to prosperity

Growth in GDP may not necessarily translate into improvements in living conditions for people. For one, GDP growth may be driven by exploitation of natural resources, without benefiting a broad group of the population. For instance, Guyana has tripled its GDP in just four years due to rapid expansion in oil production. Hence [goal: 8]also focuses on full and productive employment and decent work for all.

In general, growth in GDP is closely linked to growth in labor income. As much as 96 percent of cross-country variation in GDP growth over the past three decades can be explained by cross-country-variation in per capita labor income growth [reference: jobs report].

However, unemployment rates offer only a limited picture, especially in countries with large informal sectors and it does not capture the quality of jobs available.

For youth particularly, it is more interesting to look at the share of youth, who are not currently in employment, education or training, the so-called NEET [target: 8.6]. The share differs greatly across countries for instance when looking across income levels; as income levels increase, the share of youth who are not currently in employment, education or training falls.

Ensuring meaningful education, employment or training for youth is a monumental task for the years ahead. Unaddressed, the potential gap of 300 million jobs could destabilize societies, derail economic growth, lead to increases in cross-border migration flows, and stall poverty reduction.

It is not just about creating any job. The quality and type of job matters. Labor productivity increases as the composition of jobs change – moving from the agricultural sector to industry and services, moving from self-employment to wage employment or moving towards more complex jobs requiring greater skills.

Getting the policies right is not easy. The World Bank has recently proposed an ambitious policy framework [reference: Jobs report] to address the issues related to jobs by focusing on three pillars; production, people and places. The aim is to unlock the constraints preventing growth of productive firms, invest in education and labor force participation, and provide the required infrastructure and security.
