Measuring extreme poverty

This is a secondary subheading

The objective of [goal: 1] is to end poverty in all its forms everywhere, and [target: 1.1] calls for eradicating extreme poverty by 2030. Extreme poverty is measured as the share of people living below the international poverty line, which is set at $3.00 per person per day.[reference: foster2025] The international poverty line reflects the standard of poverty used in the poorest countries of the world, and having less than $3.00 per person per day is associated with not satisfying basic caloric needs….

Extreme poverty is ideally measured from surveys that elicit the consumption or income patterns of a representative sample of households in a country. Such surveys are not carried out in all countries every year, but under strong assumptions about how national economic growth trickles down to the poor, data from the World Bank’s Poverty and Inequality Platform can be interpolated, extrapolated back to 1950, and extrapolated forward to 2025.[reference: mahler2025a][reference: mahler2025b][reference: worldbank2025a]

The next 25 years

Where does this leave global poverty going forward? What the future holds is unclear, yet we can explore how global poverty will evolve if countries follow the paths other countries have taken over the past decades.

There are several factors behind this projected increase in global poverty if countries follow their current speed of progress. Most of the countries that over the past decades have made fast progress have nearly or completely eliminated extreme poverty, and can therefore no longer contribute to decline in the global poverty rate.

At the same time, many of the countries home to a large fraction of the extreme poor today have not experienced notable declines in extreme poverty in the past decade. The Democratic Republic of the Congo, Ethiopia, Madagascar, Mozambique, Nigeria, and Yemen are home to 40% of the global extreme poor, and none of them have seen notable progress in poverty reduction over the past 10 years. Some countries, such as Syria, Yemen, and Sudan, have seen rises in poverty due to conflict.

Yet even if all countries make progress towards curbing extreme poverty, global extreme poverty may still increase in the future. This can happen because the population growth of poorer countries is greater than the population growth of less poor countries.

Unless poor countries increase their pace of progress, past gains in global poverty reduction may soon start to be reversed.

Conclusion

Over the last 75 years, the world has witnessed staggering progress in reducing extreme poverty, largely due to countries that have managed in quick periods of time to go from high levels of poverty to virtually eliminate it. Yet progress has slowed down, and several countries appear stuck in poverty due to a combination of factors including the presence of conflict, poor governance, and vulnerability to climate change. Unless these challenges are confronted and resolved, the prospect for the future is bleak, and extreme poverty is likely to remain close to current levels in years to come.

For the countries that have ended extreme poverty, it is vital to remember that [goal: 1] calls for ending poverty in all its forms, and though extreme poverty may have been curbed, other forms of poverty may still be all too present.
