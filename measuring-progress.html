Measuring progress is not easy

Tracking progress towards the SDGs is crucial for countries to identify areas of development to prioritize and to learn from the successes and failures of other countries.

India’s challenge regarding open defecation ([target: 6.2]) offers a good example[emphasis: . ]Data revealed that India lagged behind its peers and its own target for ending open defecation before 2014. To address this, the Government of India launched the Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM), focusing on toilet construction and changing behavior regarding sanitation practices. The mission constructed over 100 million toilets by 2019, and the proportion of the population practicing open defecation fell from 33% in 2014 to 20% in 2019—a 40% drop in the span of five short years. [reference: swachh][reference: unsdg]

Cases like this illustrate how understanding countries’ progress can spearhead policy change and document achievements. Though India’s achievements were impressive, how can we quantify how well it did over this time span? It is not as trivial as it may sound.

When measuring progress towards an SDG, a common first step is to track whether a country has met a specific SDG target. This overlooks that a country like India may have made significant progress towards a target, but still fall short of it.[reference: easterly2009][reference: clemens2007] In India, despite its massive headways, more than 6% still practice open defecation, which is above the [target: 6.2][emphasis: ]target calling for an end to open defecation.[reference: unsdg]

One could instead compare progress across countries looking at the changes they have experienced. In India, we could compare the decline in the proportion of people practicing open defecation of 40%—a measure of the change in percent—with what happened in other countries. Alternatively, we could compare India’s decline of 13 percentage points (from 33% to 20%), with its peers.

Unfortunately these two simple and frequently used measures of progress often give different answers to which country has made most progress. To see this, consider the Cote d’Ivoire and Georgia’s recent progress in reducing extreme poverty ([target: 1.1]).

The future and setting targets beyond 2030

At the typical speed of development, going from very high poverty rates to a society free of extreme poverty often takes centuries. This does not only pertain to poverty, but applies to other domains of development as well. Even if countries move drastically faster than the typical speed of development, like Singapore and Korea since the 1950s, transitioning from the worst observed outcomes to some of the best easily takes 75 years.

The long road to development suggests that some SDG targets, such as complete access to services or complete elimination of deprivations, were plausible for some countries but virtually impossible for countries that are generations from reaching these levels. Globally ambitious targets are harder to achieve for poorer countries which need much faster speeds of progress to obtain the same level as countries starting from better positions. Yet success and failure has been closely associated with the outcome of these targets.[reference: waage2010]

So how should countries set realistic and aspirational targets? The speed scores explained above can be used to create targets at varying levels of ambition and with various comparator countries.

Such high speeds of development are not necessarily what countries should consider for their targets. Some countries may have less capacity to develop due to high debt levels, conflict, or other reasons, and could choose a target based on their own, slower historical pace of progress. Countries could also choose the speed of an aspirational set of countries—say, the best performers in their region—to define a target.

Challenges when measuring progress

The method presented here and most other measures of progress face limitations that are important to be aware of.

First, the method cannot be applied to all indicators. The speed method does not work for indicators where countries on expectation do not make progress. For such indicators, we have developed a complementary way of measuring progress that gives all countries a score from 0-100. This score reflects the share of historical experiences a country is outperforming.[reference: mahler2025a] For example, a shore of 80 means that a country is progressing faster than 80% of all cases we have observed historically.

Second, measuring progress is difficult when indicators change drastically from year to year, such as when measuring deaths from natural disasters. For such variables, tracking progress is challenging, as a country may have made fast progress on its emergency preparedness and adaptation to climate change, yet one year face a rare natural disaster which could suggest a lack of progress.

Third, the method requires indicators with a large amount of data in order to estimate the expected changes with sufficient accuracy. This means that indicators that lack data over a long time period or over a large set of countries will involve greater uncertainty.

Finally, the method is limited by the fact that we do not observe all countries at all stages of development. For example, to estimate how countries typically progress at high levels of development, we can only rely on information from countries that have achieved such standards. Conversely, we do not have data at low levels of development for most of the countries that are currently highly developed. This impacts the transition paths, and had such data been available, they may have looked differently.
