The persistent digital divide

Access to the internet is a cornerstone of development. It enables individuals and communities to participate in the digital economy and access vital information. The internet lowers the cost of economic and social transactions, promotes innovation, and increases efficiency and inclusion. It enables more trade, better use of capital, and greater competition, while also supporting job creation and making workers more productive. However, without complements such as well-designed regulations to foster competition, investments in people’s skills and education, and accountable institutions, digital adoption will not deliver broad-based dividends, and may even exacerbate inequality.[reference: int1]

Despite rapid progress, almost a third of the world population does not use the internet, a reflection of persistent barriers to universal access.

The share of people using the internet is measured primarily through household surveys: individuals are asked whether they used the Internet in the previous three months, from any device (computer, phone, TV, etc.) and any location (home, work, school, café). [reference: intm1] If no recent survey exists, statistical modelling is used to estimate values based on correlates like mobile-subscriptions, GDP per capita, education, and regional patterns.

Who’s left behind? Rural and gender gaps

Rural areas, in both high and low income economies, often lag behind when it comes to internet infrastructure and access. In rural settings, people are less likely than urban residents to have broadband internet access and to use online services, such as ordering food and groceries, streaming media, or using videoconferencing for work and medical care.[reference: rur1]

Another critical divide when it comes to internet access is the gender gap. In 2024, the number of men using the Internet exceeded the number of women by 189 million.[reference: gnd1] Women and girls are far less likely to use the internet regularly, especially in low-income economies (31% of men vs. 19% of women) and lower-middle-income economies (59% vs. 46%). In Bangladesh, for example, 52% of men used the internet in 2023 compared to just 37% of women. Women, especially in low-income and rural contexts, face greater barriers to digital access compared to men, particularly when it comes to affordability, infrastructure, and cultural norms.[reference: gnd2] These [emphasis: gender disparities] exclude women from economic, educational, and social opportunities offered by digital technologies.

Fixed vs. mobile broadband: complementary pathways

Globally, measuring internet use takes into account any online activity, even those requiring minimal data, such as emailing or browsing. However, data-heavy tasks, such as video streaming, cloud services, e-learning, or telemedicine, demand high-speed access or [emphasis: broadband]. There are two primary types of broadband connections: [emphasis: fixed and mobile].

Fixed and mobile broadband differ in [emphasis: capacity, reliability, cost, and use]. Fixed broadband (delivered via fiber, DSL, cable, or satellite)[reference: fxb1] offers higher, more stable speeds, and lower latency. [reference: fxb2] This makes it ideal for data-intensive activities. However, it requires costly last-mile infrastructure and is concentrated in dense, high-income areas. Mobile broadband, delivered through 4G and 5G networks, is cheaper to deploy and offers flexible wireless access, but often has higher latency, variable performance, and data caps.

In practice, the two technologies are complementary: fixed broadband underpins high-capacity national networks and enterprise use, while mobile broadband drives widespread personal connectivity and inclusion.

[emphasis: Fixed broadband] is common in many high-income countries, but nearly absent in low-income economies. In Sub-Saharan Africa, there is less than 1 fixed broadband connection per 100 people.

Extending fixed broadband to rural or low-income areas is often commercially unviable: up to 70% of costs stem from civil works like trenching, while revenues remain low due to sparse populations and limited purchasing power. This mismatch between high upfront investment and low returns discourages private operators without public support or cost-sharing. [reference: brdb1]

[emphasis: ]

[emphasis: Mobile broadband] is the main gateway to the internet for much of the world, especially in lower-income economies, as it is cheaper and faster to deploy. In Botswana and Côte d’Ivoire, where fixed broadband penetration is minimal (3.4 and 1.4 per 100 people), mobile subscriptions dominate: 135 per 100 in Botswana and 108 in Côte d’Ivoire. Over 80% of Botswana’s population is online, well above the global average of 68%.

Uneven network coverage

Mobile broadband provides wireless internet access via cellular networks (4G or 5G) through SIM-based devices (like cell phones) or fixed-wireless terminals.

[emphasis: Mobile networks] have evolved rapidly over three decades:

2G (1990s): The shift from analog to digital, enabling SMS and better voice quality.

3G: Brought mobile internet mainstream, supporting email, web browsing, and early apps.

4G: Delivered broadband-level speeds for HD video, streaming, and modern apps with lower latency.

5G: A step-change including multi-gigabit speeds, ultra-low latency, and massive device connectivity, powering IoT, smart cities, real-time automation, and advanced industrial uses.

Most of the world’s population is currently covered by 3G or 4G networks. In recent years 4G coverage has doubled, while 5G is rapidly expanding.

Least developed countries, mostly in Sub-Saharan Africa, face stark gaps in 4G and 5G coverage, with rural areas especially underserved. Sub-Saharan Africa remains the least connected region for 4G, while 5G is concentrated in high- and upper-middle-income countries. Expanding coverage in LDCs and low income countries is constrained by high infrastructure costs and weak supporting systems. Many areas lack essentials like towers, fiber backhaul, and reliable electricity, making deployment far costlier than elsewhere. [reference: infr1] Sparse rural populations drive up cost per user and reduce returns, weakening commercial incentives. [reference: infr2] Power supply is a major bottleneck: remote sites often rely on diesel or off-grid solutions, adding significant capital and maintenance costs.[reference: infr3]

Why internet speed matters

Being able to connect to the internet is the first step in taking advantage of the digital services and opportunities. However, connection speed determines the type of activities people can do once online. Faster broadband speeds support economic growth, productivity and innovation, and allow access to education and healthcare services. [reference: spd1] [reference: spd2] [reference: spd3]

Internet speed varies significantly across countries, impacting the quality of online experiences. In countries like Singapore, Chile, or United Arab Emirates median download speed, for either mobile or fixed broadband, or both, are high and supportive of a wide range of activities (over 300 Mbps), while in low-income economies, many in Sub-Saharan Africa, speeds are often very low (under 25 Mbps).

Improving speed is crucial for enabling advanced applications and services.

From coverage to connection: the usage gap

There is often a significant gap between network coverage and actual internet use. Even when broadband and especially mobile networks, technically reaches most of the population, many people remain offline due to barriers such as high costs, limited access to devices, and low levels of digital literacy. In Bangladesh, for example, 4G mobile networks covered an estimated [emphasis: 98.93%] of the population in 2023, yet only about [emphasis: 45%] of people were observed to be using the internet in the same year. This disparity highlights the difference between having potential access and being able to meaningfully go online. This disconnect highlights that infrastructure alone is not enough to achieve true digital inclusion. Understanding the reasons why people remain unconnected, even when coverage is available, is essential for designing effective and equitable interventions that turn connectivity into meaningful access and use.

Digital literacy: the key to meaningful access

Digital literacy is essential for meaningful internet use. Many people lack the skills needed to navigate online resources or communicate through mobile devices, underscoring the importance of education and training initiatives. When looking specifically at communication skills, many countries report very low levels of adoption for even the most basic tasks, such as sending text messages or using messaging apps. In some cases, [emphasis: more than half of the population has never sent a message] using a mobile phone. This gap illustrates how limited digital skills can prevent individuals from benefiting fully from available connectivity.

The device affordability barrier

One of the biggest reasons many communities remain offline is simply the cost of devices. The World Bank’s 2025 FINDEX report shows that not having enough money to buy a smartphone is the leading barrier to owning one, and without a smartphone people cannot meaningfully use the internet. This challenge is felt even more strongly by adults in the [emphasis: poorest 40% ]of the population. In low and middle income countries, recent statistics show that even a low cost smartphone [reference: devaf1] can represent about [emphasis: 18%] of an adult’s monthly income[reference: devaf2].

Improving access to affordable devices is not just helpful. It is essential for real digital inclusion.

Despite the limitations and the existing gap between coverage and connectivity, however, over the past decade, the overall trend has been largely positive. Most countries have steadily increased the share of their populations using the internet, reflecting long-term progress in expanding digital access. By 2024, around [emphasis: 54 countries] reached more than[emphasis: 90%] of their population accessing the internet. The progress seen so far suggests that, with sustained effort, closing the remaining gaps between coverage and meaningful use of the internet is within reach.

Digital inclusion in action: The SAS Brasil example

Access to the internet has become a gateway to opportunities. Being connected opens the door to better communication, access to information, and new ways of participating in daily life. When it comes to improving lives by accessing basic services such as healthcare, this connection can be transformative. The ability to send a message, make a video call, or share a health concern online creates new pathways for care, especially for people who live far from medical services. Initiatives like[emphasis: SAS Brasil] show how basic internet access can turn digital communication into a lifeline for health support.

In many rural areas of Brazil, seeing a doctor can require on average around [emphasis: 75 minutes of travel ][reference: brzst1] . For many families, that distance may keep them from timely access to medical care. Today, SAS Brasil helps bridge that gap through simple internet based telehealth services. What once required a long and costly journey can now begin with an initial text message to start a diagnosis or address a medical emergency. Using WhatsApp and secure tools, remote communities can connect with health professionals, describe their symptoms, and receive guidance without leaving their village. Local nurses often help residents navigate these consultations, making the service accessible even to those less familiar with digital tools.

This model works because communities have the infrastructure to be connected and the means to access the internet. The strategy is designed to function on low bandwidth connections and relies on tools people already use every day, making telemedicine practical and approachable. Through this setup, patients can access [emphasis: general care, specialist advice, and mental health support] promptly. Behind the scenes, the system also allows health workers to be trained and remote care to be continuously improved.

SAS Brasil’s internet based telehealth services demonstrate how access to connectivity can expand opportunities, bring essential healthcare closer to people, and strengthen communities. It shows that when the infrastructure for connection exists, digital innovation can transform lives.
