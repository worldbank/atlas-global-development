Pollution in the oceans

The ocean is central to human livelihoods, economies, and the health of the planet. The world’s oceans support nearly 144 million jobs in seafood production and coastal tourism. In 2022 aquaculture and fisheries together yielded more than 220 million tonnes of fish and seafood, valued at $470 billion. [reference: fao24] The ocean also continues to absorb about a third of total carbon dioxide emissions and plays a vital role in climate regulation. [reference: carb1]

The ocean’s ability to provide jobs, regulate climate, and supply nourishment are increasingly threatened by various forms of pollution. Pollution in the seas can take many forms but some of the most common are [emphasis: plastic pollution] and [emphasis: nutrient pollution].[reference: poll1]

Plastic pollution

The world is creating a lot of plastic, which is used in virtually all aspects of modern life. It is estimated that global use of plastic increased from 2 million tonnes (Mt) in 1950 to 460 Mt in 2019 and is projected to reach 1,231 Mt by 2060, if current trends continue.[reference: plas1]

This dramatic rise in plastic use has been matched by a surge in plastic waste. Between 2000 and 2019, plastic waste increased from 156 Mt to 353 Mt, out of which[reference: plas1] only 15% was collected for recycling and only just 9% was in fact recycled. In 2019, more than 60% of waste ended up in landfills or incinerated, and 22% or [emphasis: almost 78 million tonnes of plastic waste was mismanaged] — either dumped in uncontrolled sites or burned openly,resulting in environmental leakage.[reference: plas1]

Plastic can take hundreds of years to degrade. Over long periods of time large pieces of plastic (e.g. bottles, bags, fishing nets) begin to break down because of sunlight, waves, wind, and abrasion. [reference: plas10] Slowly they decompose into very small pieces, called [emphasis: microplastics and nanoplastics], which can be as little as one micrometer (visible only with a microscope). These [emphasis: tiny plastic particles have been found everywhere in our world], including the Antarctic, , as well in the human body, including in the liver, the brain, and the placenta.[reference: plas2][reference: plas3]

[emphasis: Plastic pollution in the ocean has a devastating impact on marine life and ecosystems]. The damage happens through plastic ingestion, where animals mistake plastic fragments or microplastics for food, leading to suffocation, nutrient deprivation, physical injury, and potential exposure to harmful chemicals; and entanglement, where larger debris like nets and ropes trap marine life, often causing injury or death.[reference: plas7]

Understanding how plastics reach the ocean is crucial to addressing the problem. [emphasis: Most of the plastic currently in the ocean is carried there by rivers]. Both large rivers and many small urban rivers act as channels for plastic to reach the ocean. Plastic waste left on land often gets carried away by rain or melting snow that runs across the ground instead of soaking in. This happens more in cities, where hard surfaces like roads and drains help debris move toward rivers and streams. Plastics near these waterways are more likely to end up in them, while those farther away may stay trapped on land. Once in rivers, plastics can be washed out to the sea during heavy rain or floods. [reference: plas4]

Globally, approximately 1,000 rivers are responsible for 80% of annual riverine plastic emissions. Most of the plastic leakage occurs through small and medium-sized urban rivers, particularly in the densely populated coastal regions of East and South Asia and in parts of West Africa.[reference: plas4]

Gyres are large systems of rotating ocean currents, driven by wind, tides, and differences in water temperature and salinity. These currents pull in and trap marine debris, especially plastic waste originating from land-based sources including coastal cities, rivers, and ships. Over time, the circular motion of the gyres causes the floating pieces of plastic to accumulate in the center of the currents. These accumulations are known as [emphasis: garbage patches]. As debris collects, larger items like fishing nets and bottles begin to break down into smaller pieces (microplastics), which then become even more entrenched in the marine environment. Because of the constant movement of currents and winds, the boundaries of these garbage patches shift over time[reference: plas5].

There are six major ocean gyres, in the North and South Pacific, North and South Atlantic, Indian Ocean, and South Atlantic gyres, all of which host significant garbage patches. The largest is the North Pacific Garbage Patch, which is located within the North Pacific Gyre.[reference: plas5]

The North Pacific Garbage Patch is a massive accumulation zone of floating plastic debris, located in the North Pacific Subtropical Gyre, between Hawaii and California. Measuring approximately 1.6 million sq km (about three times the size of France) [ref: plast5a] it contains a high concentration of large plastic items (like fishing gear and plastic containers) and a growing mass of small plastic fragments (0.5–50 mm) that have increased significantly over the years.[reference: plas6]

A study which spanned seven years of continuous sampling and monitoring, found that the concentration of smaller particles rose much faster than that of larger debris, indicating that the patch is being continually replenished by new plastic inputs, rather than just degrading material already there. This suggests that the North Pacific Garbage Patch is not just a static “island” of waste, but a dynamic zone of accumulation, shaped by ongoing pollution and complex ocean currents, which raises persistent threats to marine ecosystems.[reference: plas6]

These threats manifest in numerous ways, particularly in their impact on marine life and ecosystems. Research shows that fish exposed to both viruses and microplastic particles, especially microfibers, experience significantly higher mortality rates than those exposed to viruses alone. The findings suggest that [emphasis: microplastics may weaken fish immune responses] or increase viral susceptibility. These results underscore that plastic pollution can exacerbate disease outcomes in marine organisms, not just via ingestion or entanglement, but also lowering resilience to illness.[reference: plas8]

Evidence from human observational studies on the effects of microplastic exposure suggests that harms to reproductive health, digestive, and respiratory systems from microplastics are increasing. [reference: hum1] There is also concern that microplastics could act as carriers for chemical additives, heavy metals, or pathogens. [reference: hum2][emphasis: ]

[emphasis: Microplastics have become part of the food chain]. In a recent study, common seafood species from the U.S. West Coast, including Chinook salmon, rockfish, shrimp, and herring, were found to contain anthropogenic particles (small bits of plastic and other man-made particles). Whether caught directly or bought at stores, these particles were found in nearly all the fish samples, and confirmed that people can ingest plastics by eating seafood.[reference: plas9]

One of the most effective ways to prevent plastic from reaching the ocean is to [emphasis: strengthen municipal waste systems], in particular universal waste collection and river catchment interventions. Research shows that mismanaged waste is the primary pathway for plastic leakage, if waste is not collected or goes to unprotected or poorly managed landfills. [reference: waste1] Ensuring universal collection coverage can substantially reduce leakage and complements recycling efforts. [reference: waste2] At the same time, rivers act as major conduits for land-based plastic waste to the ocean, making interception technologies (such litter traps, floating booms, and stormwater filtration systems) which catch plastics at key leakage points (streets, drains, rivers) before they reach the ocean [reference: waste3], highly effective when deployed at mapped leakage “hotspots”. [reference: waste4]

Yet, plastic pollution is only one aspect of the broader challenge facing our oceans. Less visible but also highly damaging is nutrient pollution, which stems from excess nitrogen and phosphorus entering marine ecosystems. Nutrient overload alters the chemistry of ocean waters, fuelling algal blooms, depleting oxygen, and threatening marine life in far-reaching ways.

Nutrient pollution

Nutrients—chemical substances—are necessary to the lives of people, plants, animals, and all other living organisms.

[emphasis: Nitrogen ](N) [reference: nutr3] and [emphasis: phosphorus ](P) [reference: nutr4] are two of the [emphasis: most vital nutrients for organisms]. While [emphasis: essential for sustaining life] and enabling the growth and functioning of organisms, nitrogen and phosphorus are also two nutrients most frequently identified as primary drivers of [emphasis: nutrient pollution] when they [emphasis: accumulate in the environment] in excessive amounts. [reference: nutr2]

Since the beginning of industrialization, inputs of nitrogen and phosphorus resulting from human activity (i.e, anthropogenic inputs) into the biosphere have increased substantially. Since the 1860s, anthropogenic nitrogen inputs have risen roughly nine-fold and phosphorus inputs about three-fold [reference: nutr1].

Anthropogenic nitrogen and phosphorus result from a wide array of human activities, including intensive agriculture, industrial production, energy generation, and urban expansion, each of which alters natural nutrient cycles and amplifies the risk of environmental accumulation and transport.

[emphasis: Most nitrogen pollution comes from human activities, such as using chemical fertilizers on crops, managing animal waste, and burning fossil fuels].[reference: nutr5] [reference: nutr6] Roughly 22% of human-derived nitrogen accumulates in soils and plants, while about 35% ends up in the ocean. [reference: nutr7] Farming practices and city growth are major drivers of nitrogen pollution in coastal waters.[reference: nutr8]

[emphasis: Phosphorus ]originates from both natural sources—like the phosphorus already in soil and particles carried by the air , and from human activities.[reference: nutr9] Excess phosphorus entering lakes and rivers, and ultimately flowing into the ocean, commonly derives from [emphasis: industrial waste], [emphasis: construction activities], [emphasis: urban runoff], [emphasis: sewage], and [emphasis: agricultural drainage].[reference: nutr10] [reference: nutr11]

Deforestation and changes in land use, such as clearing forests and disturbing soil, can reduce the environment’s ability to absorb nutrients. This often leads to increased soil erosion and runoff, which carries nutrients into rivers, lakes, and oceans.[reference: nutr12] [reference: nutr13]

When excessive amounts of nitrogen and phosphorus enter rivers, lakes, or coastal waters, they act like fertilizer for microscopic life, spurring a burst of algae and phytoplankton growth [reference: nutr14]. This sudden bloom fills the water with chlorophyll-a—the pigment that gives these organisms their green color and powers photosynthesis. Because of this, tracking chlorophyll-a has become one of the clearest ways to understand how nutrient pollution shapes freshwater and marine ecosystems (see, for example, [reference: nutr15] and [reference: nutr16]).

As the blooms spread, the water surface can take on a thick, greenish film. Light struggles to reach below, and underwater plants begin to fade in the shadows. Each day, as the algae grow and die, oxygen levels rise and fall in sharp cycles [reference: nutr17].

As blooms intensify, decomposition of excess organic matter further consumes oxygen, destabilizing the natural balance of aquatic ecosystems. Over time, this sequence of changes can transform diverse, functioning water bodies into degraded systems with reduced biodiversity and impaired ecological resilience. [reference: nutr18]

Coastal eutrophication happens when too many nutrients build up in the water. [emphasis: In coastal areas, excessive nitrogen and phosphorus ][emphasis: speed up ][emphasis: algal growth, which ][emphasis: disrupts the natural balance.] This overgrowth reduces oxygen, harms habitats, and changes how species interact. For example, seagrass beds and coral reefs,ecosystems that rely on clear, oxygen-rich waters,decline when smothered by algae or exposed to prolonged hypoxia. [reference: nutr19] Fish populations diminish as spawning grounds are damaged, while marine animals struggle to adapt to shifting conditions. [reference: nutr20]

Over time, these changes ripple through food webs, causing biodiversity loss and weakening the resilience of coastal ecosystems. By documenting and monitoring eutrophication “hot spots”, policymakers and resource managers can target interventions that restore ecological balance, protect habitats, and maintain the productivity of marine resources in coastal waters and beyond.

[emphasis: Harmful algal blooms] are among the [emphasis: most visible and damaging outcomes of nutrient over-enrichment], with serious impacts on both people and economies. As these blooms spread, they deplete the oxygen in the water, which often results in large scale fish kills, devastating fisheries and threatening food security. Some algal species also release potent toxins, which can accumulate in seafood, contaminate drinking water, and become airborne, creating health risks ranging from skin irritation to respiratory illness and, in severe cases, neurological damage. [reference: nutr21] [emphasis: These risks disproportionately affect vulnerable populations such as children and the elderly].

Beyond health impacts, harmful blooms have heavy economic costs: fisheries and aquaculture suffer losses from contaminated stocks, coastal tourism declines as beaches and waterways become unsafe, and public health systems face increased burdens from outbreaks linked to polluted water (see, for instance, [reference: nutr22]). The combined ecological, social, and economic damages underscore why controlling nutrient pollution is essential for both environmental sustainability and human well-being.

Preventing and reducing marine pollution, in particular from land-based activities, including marine debris and nutrient pollution is also the aim of SDG Target 14.1 which aims to bring attention and monitor progress in addressing these challenges.
