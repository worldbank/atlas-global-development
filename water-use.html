Ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all

Introduction

Water is essential to life. To ensure our health and well-being, we need reliable access to clean, safe drinking water at home. This necessitates both a dependable and sufficient supply available, as well as rigorous maintenance and quality assurance. While reliable access is often taken for granted in high-income countries, 2.2 billion people—27 percent of the world’s population—still lack drinking water services classified as “safely managed.”

The global number of people without “safely managed” drinking water services is

2.2  billion people

Among them, 700 million of those people (10 percent of the global population) do not even have access to water at the “basic” level of service—meaning they rely on contaminated sources or spend hours collecting water each day.

Even for those with access to the highest level of service, water quality is not always guaranteed. Natural disasters such as floods, along with poorly maintained infrastructure, can compromise water quality. Contaminated drinking water poses serious health risks, especially for young children and infants, with exposure to harmful chemicals potentially causing long-term risks [reference: chemical_risks] for health and cognitive development. These challenges occur across the world — including high-income countries, where contamination is typically temporary but still disruptive — making water quality a global concern.

Poor water quality carries significant human and economic costs. Unsafe water leads to illness, both in the short and long term, increasing healthcare expenses and straining household resources. Children who fall sick miss school, hindering learning [reference: learning] and reducing future human capital[reference: human_capital]. Adults face lost productivity due to illness, caregiving responsibilities, or premature death, which affects household incomes and labor markets. Over time, these individual impacts accumulate, weakening a country’s human capital base and slowing economic growth[reference: econ_growth].

Classification of drinking water service level

Definition of safely managed drinking water

SDG target 6.1 aims to achieve universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water for all. Drinking water services are classified into five levels[reference: water_levels]: Safely managed, [emphasis: Basic], [emphasis: Limited], [emphasis: Unimproved], and [emphasis: Surface water]. [emphasis: Safely managed drinking water]—the highest level and the indicator for SDG 6.1.1—is defined as the proportion of the population using an improved water source, that is: [emphasis: accessible on premises[reference: premises], available when needed, and free from fecal and priority chemical contamination]. Improved water sources (such as piped water, protected wells, or packaged water) are designed to protect against contamination, but not all automatically ensure safety[reference: improved]. ￼ Improved sources that fulfill all three components are classified as safely managed.

Estimating safely managed drinking water access

To meet the definition of safely managed drinking water (SDG indicator 6.1.1), people must use an improved water source that is [emphasis: accessible on premises, available when needed, and free from contamination]. Ideally, estimates would measure all three components for the same population. In practice, however, data for accessibility, availability, and water quality often come from different surveys and sources, making it difficult to determine exactly how many people meet all three criteria simultaneously.

To address this challenge, the methodology applies a conservative approach: the [emphasis: minimum value among the three components] is used to estimate the share of the population with safely managed drinking water. This ensures that the estimate does not overstate access to fully safe and reliable water services.

Limiting factors of safely managed drinking water

A limiting factor is a constraint that holds back progress. For safely managed drinking water, it refers to the lowest value among the three components: accessibility, availability, or quality. Across 128 countries with available data (excluding the 31 countries that have already achieved more than 99 percent safely managed coverage), water quality is the most common limiting factor, affecting 62 countries (47 percent), followed by accessibility in 43 countries (38 percent) and availability in 23 countries (14 percent).

Accessibility is a major challenge in many Sub-Saharan African countries, such as the Central African Republic, Chad, and the Democratic Republic of Congo, where access to water on premises remains limited. By contrast, water quality is the limiting factor in several Pacific Island nations, including Tuvalu and Kiribati, as well as in parts of South and Southeast Asia, such as Nepal, Lao PDR, and Cambodia, where data show low compliance with water quality standards.

[emphasis: Note:] This indicator can only be computed when water quality data are available along with at least one other component (accessibility or availability). Among the 159 countries with data, all have measures for quality and accessibility, but 57 lack data on availability.

Focusing on water quality

Water quality data: sources, challenges and innovations

Water quality is both a critical determinant of safely managed drinking water and an essential component of SDG 6.1.1. While data availability has improved in recent years, significant gaps remain: in 2024, 32 percent of countries still lacked water quality data making it difficult to fully assess safely managed services worldwide.

Administrative systems —such as national water regulators and monitoring agencies (e.g., the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency[reference: EPA] or equivalent national bodies)— that regularly monitor water quality are the preferred source of data. In many low- and middle-income countries, water monitoring systems are often lacking, which makes household surveys a crucial source of information about water quality. While these surveys may only capture a moment in time and could underestimate the extent of contamination, they remain essential for understanding the risks of public health. By 2022, nearly 50 countries[reference: MICS] had incorporated water quality modules into household surveys such as Multiple Indicator Cluster Survey (MICS) and Living Standards Measurement Study (LSMS) . Even with these surveys , coverage may be limited- as testing requires specialized training, equipment, and consumables—posing financial and logistical challenges.

E.coli contamination

Tracking contamination and health impacts

Untreated human excreta is one of the most serious sources of water pollution. For monitoring SDG indicator 6.1.1, drinking water must be free from Escherichia coli (E. coli), the key indicator of fecal contamination. Water contaminated by human feces can lead to various acute diseases and sometimes pose life-threatening risks, particularly to infants and young children who are most vulnerable as their immune systems are still developing. Every year, half a million people die from diarrheal diseases due to drinking unsafe water, of which an estimated 273,000 (54 percent)[reference:burden_disease] are children younger than five years. Other health risks from contaminated water include pneumonia, urinary tract infections, and sepsis.

Level of E.Coli contamination

To assess fecal contamination through household surveys, the presence of E. coli is measured in a 100 mL water sample. According to WHO guidelines, water is considered: low risk if it contains fewer than 1 E. coli per 100 mL, medium risk if it contains 1–10, high risk if 11–100, and very high risk if it exceeds 100 E. coli per 100 mL.

Household water treatment

Household water treatment is an important public health intervention to improve drinking water quality, particularly for those without access to safely managed services.

Heating water to a rolling boil is recommended as a highly effective method for killing bacteria and other pathogens by WHO guidelines[reference: WHO_guide]. Other methods such as chlorination, filtration, or solar disinfection are also considered appropriate options. These measures are included in household surveys.

Yet, survey data show that in many cases, households that report treating their water still have measurable contamination. This indicates that household water treatment, as reported, does not guarantee protection from E. coli contamination. Researchers[reference: ecoli_research] attribute this gap to inconsistent or incorrect treatment, reliance on ineffective technologies[reference: ineffective_treat], or biases in self-reporting.

E.coli contamination: urban-rural gap

Urban–rural disparities in water contamination are widespread. In most countries with available survey data, rural populations face significantly higher risks from contaminated drinking water than their urban counterparts. For example, in Georgia, 53 percent of rural residents use water sources classified as high risk, compared to just 5 percent of urban residents. Similar patterns are seen in Côte d'Ivoire and the Republic of Congo, where the proportion of rural dwellers using high-risk water sources is more than twice that of urban dwellers—72 percent versus 32 percent and 75 percent versus 36 percent, respectively.

Chemical contaminants in water

Beyond microbial risks, chemical contaminants such as arsenic, fluoride, nitrates, and heavy metals also pose serious long-term health threats. Under SDG 6.1.1, arsenic (WHO guideline: 10 ppb) and fluoride (1.5 ppm) are priority concerns. Fluoride at low concentrations strengthens teeth, but excessive exposure[reference: fluoride] can cause fluorosis—ranging from dental discoloration to severe skeletal fluorosis, which may result in osteosclerosis, calcification of tendons and ligaments, joint stiffness, and bone deformities. Arsenic contamination is likewise harmful, with prolonged exposure[reference: arsenic_exposure] associated with skin lesions, cancers, cardiovascular disease, and cognitive impairment.

Drinking water contaminated with arsenic in Bangladesh

Arsenic is a naturally occurring element found in the earth’s crust. About 140 million people[reference: arsenic_pop] in at least 70 countries consume drinking water containing arsenic above WHO’s guideline. The largest numbers of people exposed are in Bangladesh and India[reference: arsenic_bgd_ind]. Bangladesh faces the world’s most severe arsenic crisis: since the 1990s, millions have been exposed, leading to cancers, skin disease, and adverse pregnancy outcomes.

Drinking water sources contaminated with arsenic

In Bangladesh, tubewells—the most common drinking water source—are more likely to contain arsenic than piped or surface water. Widely installed in the 1970s–80s to replace unsafe surface water that often carried germs, they have since become a major source of contamination. Today, about 19 percent of the population consumes water above WHO’s 10 ppb guideline, and 12 percent above the national standard of 50 ppb.

Districts with highest exposure to arsenic contaminated water

Sunamganj, Gopalganj, and Cumilla districts have the highest proportion of the population exposed to arsenic above WHO’s 10 ppb guideline—more than half of residents in each district. When applying the national standard of 50 ppb, these same districts remain the most affected, with 48 percent (Cumilla), 45 percent (Gopalganj), and 48 percent (Sunamganj) of residents using contaminated water.

In Cumilla and Chandpur, over one-third of the population uses water with arsenic concentrations exceeding 200 ppb—four times the national limit.

People who are at highest risks

The government’s initiatives and programs to solve arsenic issues

Since the 1990s, the Government of Bangladesh has introduced a wide range of programs to address arsenic contamination in drinking water. These efforts have included nationwide screening of tubewells, the installation of arsenic-safe water sources such as piped systems drawing on safe surface or deep groundwater, and the use of household and community filtration technologies. Public awareness campaigns have also been carried out, alongside health sector initiatives to identify and manage arsenic-related illnesses.

Despite these initiatives, arsenic contamination remains a pressing challenge. Millions of people continue to rely on unsafe water because arsenic-free options—such as deep wells, piped systems, or rainwater harvesting—are often unavailable, unaffordable, or too distant[reference: arsenic_reason]. Research[reference: arsenic_research] indicates that limited resources, gaps in local information, and the sheer number of people and wells affected further complicate mitigation efforts. Countries like Bangladesh face the dual burden of microbial risks and arsenic contamination, a problem that is particularly difficult for low- and middle-income countries with constrained resources.

CONCLUSION

Safely managed drinking water remains a cornerstone of human health and development. While global access has progressed over the past two decades, deep inequalities persist across regions, countries, income groups, and between rural and urban areas. A major challenge is that in many low- and middle-income countries, drinking water is still contaminated by E. coli or harmful chemicals such as arsenic.

The World Bank has supported countries through a range of projects to improve water quality. For example, the LSMS team, working with the WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply, Sanitation and Hygiene (JMP), has piloted innovative approaches[reference: LSMS] that combine household surveys with machine learning models to predict contamination risks, helping governments monitor water safety more effectively in data-scarce environments. At the same time, strengthening sanitation and hygiene systems is critical, but sanitation programs, in particular, remain chronically underfunded[reference: SWS] in many countries. Without adequate sanitation and hand hygiene, investments in water supply are undermined by contamination, perpetuating cycles of disease.

As Nobel laureate Michael Kremer emphasizes, the link between water quality, access to safe drinking water, and child survival is especially strong in low- and middle-income countries. Evidence shows[reference: Kremer] that improved water treatment can reduce child mortality by 25–32 percent, with the greatest benefits for infants and young children.
